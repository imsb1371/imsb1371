There is a persistent tension in my field between the richness of qualitative inquiry and the scalability demands of modern science. A single semi-structured interview, properly analyzed, can yield insights that no survey of 10,000 respondents could surface. But interviewing 10,000 people is, for most research teams, simply impossible.

## The Scale Problem

The standard response has been triangulation — use qualitative methods for depth and quantitative methods for breadth, then synthesize. This works, but it comes at a cost: the two streams of evidence rarely inform each other in real time.

## What Computational Methods Offer

Over the past three years, I've been exploring a different model: using NLP not to replace qualitative analysis, but to guide where to look. By running transformer-based topic models over interview transcripts before human coding begins, we can surface unexpected themes that might otherwise be lost.

The result is a kind of *augmented grounded theory* — preserving the interpretive depth of traditional qualitative analysis while leveraging computation to manage scale.

## Honest Caveats

This approach is not without risks. Researchers trained primarily in quantitative methods may over-trust computational outputs. The NLP models themselves carry biases. And the seductive precision of cluster probabilities can create a false sense of rigor in what is, at its core, an interpretive process.
